Colorado
Denver – The Mile-High City, Colorado’s capital and a hub of craft breweries, art museums (e.g. Denver Art Museum), and nearby Rocky Mountain adventures.
Colorado Springs – Home to Pikes Peak (“America’s Mountain”) and Garden of the Gods park (towering red rock formations).  This mountain-front city offers hiking, cycling, and views of the Front Range.
Aspen – A legendary ski resort town in the Rocky Mountains.  Aspen’s high-end shops, music festivals, and Aspen Snowmass resort make it a year-round draw for outdoor sports and culture.

Connecticut
Hartford – The state capital on the Connecticut River, home to the Mark Twain House and Wadsworth Atheneum Museum (the nation’s oldest public art museum).  Downtown also features Capitol tours and riverfront parks.
New Haven – Home of Yale University, with historic campus architecture and college-town charm.  New Haven’s food scene (especially original New Haven pizza) and Yale galleries/theaters draw cultural travelers.
Mystic – A picturesque coastal village known for the Mystic Seaport Museum (nation’s leading maritime museum) and Mystic Aquarium.  The harborfront village and Mystic Pizza (famous in film) are regional favorites.
Greenwich – Affluent town on Long Island Sound known for upscale shopping and mansions.  Attractions include Greenwich Avenue (boutiques), Audubon center at Greenwich Point Park, and historic sites in a New England coastal setting.
Delaware
Wilmington – Delaware’s largest city with historic downtown and riverfront.  Highlights include the Riverfront area, tours of the historic DuPont estates, and the Hagley Museum (Industrial Revolution history).
Dover – The state capital with a restored Colonial courthouse and the Dover International Speedway.  Nearby Dover Air Force Base’s air museum is a draw for aviation enthusiasts.
Rehoboth Beach – A popular summer resort town with a classic boardwalk, boutique shops, and wide sandy beaches.  Its family-friendly amusements and tax-free outlet shopping make it a regional draw.
Lewes – A historic port town known as the “First Town in the First State.”  Its charming downtown, beaches, and the Cape May–Lewes Ferry make it a scenic and historic seaside destination.
Florida
Miami – Famous for its vibrant South Beach, Art Deco architecture, and lively nightlife (Ocean Drive).  The multicultural city’s Cuban and Caribbean influences show in its cuisine and Calle Ocho’s Little Havana.
Orlando – Known worldwide for its theme parks (Walt Disney World, Universal Orlando, etc.), Orlando is a top family travel destination.  Beyond parks, visitors find water parks, dinner shows, and resort spas.
Tampa – A Gulf Coast city featuring the historic Ybor City district (Cuban cigar heritage), Busch Gardens theme park, and the revitalized Riverwalk downtown.  Nearby beaches (St. Pete, Clearwater) add to its appeal.
Key West – The southernmost city in the continental U.S., famous for its colorful Caribbean vibe.  Visitors enjoy the pastel “conch” houses, snorkeling reefs, the Ernest Hemingway Home, and legendary sunsets at Mallory Square.
Georgia
Atlanta – The state capital and a transportation hub (“The ATL”) known for its Civil Rights heritage (Martin Luther King Jr. Center), the Georgia Aquarium, and a booming food and arts scene.  It’s also home to a major film industry presence.
Savannah – A picturesque historic city on the coast, beloved for its Spanish moss-draped squares, antebellum architecture, and haunting beauty.  Riverfront cobblestone walks, ghost tours, and Southern cuisine make it a romantic destination.
Augusta – Famous as home of the annual Masters Golf Tournament (Augusta National Golf Club).  The city’s riverside Riverwalk, and historic downtown reflect its 18th-century origins.
Athens – A lively college town (University of Georgia) with a strong music and arts reputation.  Downtown is packed with bars, bands, and restaurants, while the surrounding countryside offers hiking and state parks.

Coeur d’Alene – A resort city on Lake Coeur d’Alene, celebrated for its crystal-clear lake, water sports, and floating boardwalk.  It’s a popular summer getaway with golf courses and forest trails.
Sun Valley (Ketchum) – Famous ski resort and retreat for the rich and famous.  The Sawtooth Mountains surround Sun Valley, which also hosts summer music festivals and extensive Nordic trails.
Idaho Falls – A riverfront city that serves as a gateway to Yellowstone (just over the border).  It’s known for the Idaho Falls Greenbelt and the nearby Island Park resort area.

Indiana
Indianapolis – The capital city, known for the Indianapolis Motor Speedway (home of the Indy 500) and a growing cultural scene (museum campus on White River).  The downtown Canal Walk and bustling sports venues add to its appeal.
Bloomington – Home to Indiana University, this town offers a college town atmosphere with arts, music, and campus tours.  Nearby attractions include Lake Monroe and outdoor parks.
South Bend – Known primarily for the University of Notre Dame campus and its Golden Dome.  The renovated downtown riverfront and museums (e.g., Studebaker National Museum) are also draws.
Brown County (Nashville) – A scenic artsy town nestled in rolling hills, famous for autumn foliage.  Brown County State Park provides hiking and horseback trails, and Nashville is known for its galleries and craft shops.
Iowa
Des Moines – The state capital, growing as a cultural hub.  It boasts a riverwalk downtown, the Iowa State Capitol building with its golden dome, and annual state fair in August.
Iowa City – Home of the University of Iowa and the Old Capitol building.  It has a lively literary and arts scene, historic brick streets, and access to the scenic Devonian Fossil Gorge.
Dubuque – A river town on the Mississippi River, known for its steep bluff-top views.  Attractions include the National Mississippi River Museum and Aquarium and the Fenelon Place Elevator (shortest, steepest funicular).
Cedar Rapids – The second-largest city, with a strong arts community.  The Cedar Rapids Museum of Art houses Grant Wood’s “American Gothic,” and the revitalized Czech Village offers breweries and festivals.
Kansas
Wichita – The state’s largest city, featuring the Keeper of the Plains sculpture at the river confluence and the Sedgwick County Zoo.  Wichita’s Old Town district has shops, nightlife, and riverfront parks.
Kansas City (KS) – One half of the Kansas City metro, known for jazz, barbecue, and fountains.  Visitors often cross into KC, Missouri, but Kansas’s side has sports venues and cultural districts.
Lawrence – A bustling college town (University of Kansas) with a vibrant arts scene.  Massachusetts Street is lined with boutiques, cafes, and the historic Allen Fieldhouse.
Topeka – The capital city, home to the Kansas State Capitol building (known for its gold-painted dome) and the Brown v. Board of Education historic site (civil rights landmark).
Kentucky
Louisville – Known worldwide for the Kentucky Derby (Churchill Downs).  The city also offers bourbon distillery tours, a lively waterfront, and historic neighborhoods like Old Louisville (Victorian homes).
Lexington – “The Horse Capital of the World,” surrounded by horse farms and bourbon distilleries.  Visitors tour the Kentucky Horse Park, enjoy downtown’s Eatery scene, and drive the scenic Bourbon Trail.
Bowling Green – Home of the National Corvette Museum and gateway to Mammoth Cave National Park (the world’s longest cave system) nearby.  The historic river town also features vintage railroad attractions.
Paducah – Located at the confluence of the Ohio and Tennessee rivers, Paducah’s Lower Town is a National Historic District filled with artist studios and museums.  It’s also designated a UNESCO Creative City for crafts (quiltmaking).

Bangor – Known as the home of author Stephen King and a riverfront city.  Bangor’s Cultu garden ship and historic downtown supplement nearby nature areas; it’s also an access point for freshwater lakes and trail networks.
Maryland
Baltimore – A historic port city with the vibrant Inner Harbor (National Aquarium, historic ships).  Cultural attractions include the Baltimore Museum of Art and the birthplace of Babe Ruth (Owens Park neighborhood).
Annapolis – The colonial capital and home of the U.S. Naval Academy.  Visitors enjoy 18th-century brick streets, sailing on Chesapeake Bay, and tours of William Paca’s Georgian mansion.
Ocean City – A classic Maryland beach resort on the Atlantic.  Its long wooden boardwalk, amusement piers, and beaches are a summertime tradition for families.
Frederick – A small city at the foot of the Catoctin Mountains.  It offers a well-preserved historic downtown, access to Civil War battlefields, and the nearby Harry Truman National Historic Site in nearby Pennsylvania.
Massachusetts
Boston – The historic cradle of the nation.  Tour the Freedom Trail to see Faneuil Hall, Paul Revere’s House, and the USS Constitution.  Boston’s neighborhoods (North End’s Italian, Back Bay’s brownstones) and Fenway Park (Red Sox) round out its iconic status.
Cape Cod (Provincetown) – The Outer Cape’s tip (Provincetown) is a vibrant seaside town with artist colonies and whale-watch tours.  The Cape’s beaches, lighthouse-lined villages, and scenic drive on Route 6 appeal to all ages.
Salem – Famous for its 1692 Witch Trials, Salem capitalizes on its history with museums and tours (witch trial reenactments, the House of the Seven Gables).  Its harbor and colonial architecture also attract visitors.
Martha’s Vineyard – An island off the coast (reachable by ferry) known for its charming towns (Oak Bluffs, Edgartown), beaches, and summer festivals.  The whole island is a tourist draw for its relaxed seaside atmosphere.
Michigan
Detroit – The Motor City, rebounding with new investments.  Major draws include the Detroit Institute of Arts, the Motown Museum, and the revitalized riverfront.  Its auto heritage and music history (Motown) give it cultural cachet.
Grand Rapids – Known for craft breweries and ArtPrize (annual art competition).  Also home to the Frederik Meijer Gardens (botanical gardens/art museum) and close to Lake Michigan beaches in the summer.
Traverse City – A resort town on Grand Traverse Bay, famous for cherries and wineries.  Grand Traverse Lighthouse and nearby Sleeping Bear Dunes National Lakeshore make it a scenic destination.
Mackinac Island – A car-free island in Lake Huron, known for Victorian charm (no cars are allowed).  Visitors bike or ride horse-drawn carriages around Fort Mackinac, shoreline bluffs, and the historic Grand Hotel.
Minnesota

Duluth – A port city on Lake Superior’s western tip, known for the Aerial Lift Bridge and Lakewalk.  Duluth is a gateway to Superior Hiking Trail and nearby North Shore attractions like Split Rock Lighthouse.
Bloomington (Mall of America) – Home to the huge Mall of America shopping and entertainment complex.  (It’s technically a suburb of Minneapolis but a major draw in itself.)
Boundary Waters (Voyageurs NP area) – A canoe country wilderness on the Canadian border.  Though remote, entry points near Ely or International Falls attract adventurers seeking the quintessential Minnesota lake-canoe experience.
Mississippi

Biloxi – On the Gulf Coast, known for its casinos and beaches.  Biloxi’s Maritime & Seafood Industry Museum and historic lighthouse reflect its fishing and Coast Guard heritage.
Oxford – A lively college town (University of Mississippi) with a charming courthouse square.  Famed author William Faulkner’s home is here, and local music venues and eateries capture Southern culture.
Natchez – A river town with dozens of antebellum mansions.  Natchez offers carriage tours and Civil War history, as well as a picturesque view of the Mississippi River bluffs.
Missouri
St. Louis – Known for the iconic Gateway Arch on the Mississippi River.  The city also boasts a major zoo, Forest Park, and historic neighborhoods like Soulard (jazz) and The Hill (Italian cuisine).

Hannibal – The boyhood home of Mark Twain, situated on the Mississippi River.  Visitors tour the Mark Twain Boyhood Home & Museum and paddleboat rides, immersing in the tales of Tom Sawyer.
Montana
Billings – Montana’s largest city, near the Yellowstone River.  It’s a regional hub with a charming downtown (Moss Mansion) and access to nearby natural areas like Pictograph Cave.
Missoula – A university town surrounded by mountains and rivers.  Known for its outdoor recreation (rock climbing at Mount Sentinel, rivers for rafting), it also has a strong craft beer scene.
West Yellowstone – The gateway town on Montana’s edge of Yellowstone National Park.  It bustles with visitors stopping by the Grizzly & Wolf Discovery Center and tours into the Park’s geysers and wildlife.
Glacier (Whitefish) – Town near Glacier National Park’s west entrance.  Whitefish is a ski resort community with a vibrant downtown and access to park attractions like Going-to-the-Sun Road (spectacular mountain highway).
Nebraska
Omaha – The largest city, known for the Henry Doorly Zoo (world-class exhibits), a revitalized Old Market district (shops, galleries), and the Durham Museum in a historic train station.
Lincoln – The capital city, with the Nebraska State Capitol building (iconic art-deco tower) and the University of Nebraska campus.  Haymarket District is a bustling downtown area with restaurants and venues.
Kearney – A central Nebraska town known for the Archway Monument (a museum spanning I-80 celebrating westward expansion) and the nearby Sandhill crane migration each spring.
Scottsbluff – Home to Scotts Bluff National Monument (tall bluffs that guided pioneers).  The city serves as a base for exploring western Nebraska’s Badlands and river valleys.

New Hampshire

New Jersey
Atlantic City – New Jersey’s resort city on the Jersey Shore.  Known for its Boardwalk, casinos, and outlets, it’s also famous for Steel Pier amusement rides and annual Miss America pageants.

Carlsbad – A small town near Carlsbad Caverns National Park.  Visitors explore the massive limestone cave system (attraction of bat flights at sunset) and the Carlsbad Caverns museum before returning to town’s hospitality.

North Dakota

Ohio
Cleveland – On Lake Erie, home to the Rock & Roll Hall of Fame and the Cleveland Museum of Art.  Its redeveloped waterfront (West Side Market) and neighborhood eateries make it a rising vacation stop in the Midwest.
Cincinnati – Along the Ohio River, known for its 19th-century Over-the-Rhine district (striped between modern cafes and breweries).  Its zoo (one of the oldest) and historic Roebling Suspension Bridge are notable sights.
Columbus – The state capital and college town (Ohio State University).  Attractions include the Ohio Statehouse, German Village (historic brick neighborhood with shops), and the Scioto Mile park system downtown.
Hocking Hills – A region best known for Hocking Hills State Park (Rock House, Old Man’s Cave).  While not a city, nearby Logan and Ash Cave host cabin resorts and nature lodges, making it a popular nature retreat.
Oklahoma
Oklahoma City – The capital with the National Cowboy & Western Heritage Museum and the Oklahoma City National Memorial (OKC bombing site).  Bricktown (downtown entertainment district on a canal) and a revitalized riverfront park draw visitors.
Tulsa – Known for its Art Deco architecture and thriving arts scene (Tulsa Art Deco Museum).  Tulsa also has cultural attractions like the Philbrook Museum (art in gardens) and the Woody Guthrie Center (folk music).
Lawton (Mount Scott) – Near Fort Sill in southwestern Oklahoma.  Adjacent Wichita Mountains Wildlife Refuge offers free-roaming bison and peaks like Mount Scott, making Lawton a base for wildlife hiking.
Quapaw (Beavers Bend) – While Quapaw, OK is across the border, the eastern Oklahoma side includes Beavers Bend State Park near Broken Bow.  The area is known for mountain lake resorts and forests – a surprisingly scenic getaway in Oklahoma.
Oregon
Portland – The largest Oregon city with a quirky, bike-friendly vibe.  Famous for its food trucks, microbreweries, and Powell’s Books (world’s largest independent bookstore).  Nearby forested parks (Washington Park, Multnomah Falls) are a quick drive away.
Crater Lake (Prospect) – The visitor gateway town to Crater Lake National Park.  Crater Lake’s deep blue water in a volcanic caldera is one of Oregon’s top natural attractions, framed by steep rim trails.
Bend – A Central Oregon outdoor-recreation hub near the Cascade Mountains.  Known for its craft breweries, nearby ski resort (Mt. Bachelor), and access to high-desert rivers for kayaking and fishing.
Cannon Beach – A coastal town famous for Haystack Rock (a 235-foot sea stack) and wide sandy beaches.  It’s a classic Pacific Northwest seaside village with art galleries and beachcombing (often with stormwatching from fall to spring).
Pennsylvania
Philadelphia – The nation’s first capital, rich in history (Independence Hall, Liberty Bell).  Philly also has top museums (Philadelphia Museum of Art and its “Rocky steps”), and a famous cheesesteak sandwich scene in Reading Terminal Market.
Pittsburgh – Known for its three rivers and steel-industry history.  Its revitalized riverfront (Three Rivers Heritage Trail) and the Andy Warhol Museum are highlights.  Pittsburgh’s skyline and bridges are world-famous visuals.
Lancaster (Amish Country) – A rural area center, Lancaster city is surrounded by rolling farmland.  Visitors experience Pennsylvania Dutch heritage on buggy tours, farmstead eateries, and the Central Market (oldest farmers’ market in the U.S.).
Gettysburg – The town surrounding Gettysburg National Military Park (site of a pivotal Civil War battle).  Its historic buildings and battlefield tours attract history buffs, and special events commemorate Lincoln’s speech.
Rhode Island
Providence – The capital on Narragansett Bay, known for its historic College Hill (home to Brown University) and WaterFire art installation (bonfires on the river).  Its Federal Hill neighborhood offers acclaimed Italian dining.
Newport – A seaside city famed for its Gilded Age mansions (e.g. The Breakers).  The Cliff Walk (ocean-edge path past gilded estates) and sailing culture make Newport a summer tourist hotspot.
Block Island – A small island off the Rhode Island coast, accessible by ferry.  Known for dramatic Mohegan Bluffs, bike rentals, and unspoiled beaches.  It’s a quiet resort escape for coastal hiking and sunbathing.
Narragansett – A classic Rhode Island beach town.  The town’s long Narragansett Town Beach and nearby Castle Hill Lighthouse draw summer visitors, and it serves as a base for exploring the southern Ocean Drive scenic route.
South Carolina
Charleston – A historic port city known for its well-preserved antebellum architecture and cobblestone streets.  Southern charm abounds from horse-drawn carriages to waterfront parks, with notable plantation tours outside the city�.

Myrtle Beach – A long stretch of wide sandy shores on the Grand Strand.  Known for family-friendly boardwalk amusements, golf courses, and beachfront resorts, it’s a popular East Coast vacation destination.
Columbia – The capital and home to the University of South Carolina.  Its Riverbanks Zoo & Garden and the refurbished Congaree Vista dining district attract visitors.  The South Carolina State Museum and government buildings provide cultural stops.
Greenville – A picturesque upstate city at the foothills of the Blue Ridge.  Its revitalized downtown along the Reedy River (with a waterfall park) and proximity to bike paths make it an outdoor-oriented spot.  The city regularly ranks highly for livability.
South Dakota
Keystone (Mount Rushmore) – Keystone is the gateway town to Mount Rushmore National Memorial (the famous presidential heads).  Visitors stay in Keystone and take the short drive up to see the monumental sculpture and nearby Lakota-themed attractions.
Rapid City – The main lodging city for Black Hills and Mount Rushmore.  It has a quirky downtown (public art walk) and nearby attractions like Badlands National Park and Custer State Park (wildlife loop).
Badlands (Interior) – The town of Interior is a hub for Badlands National Park.  The park’s jagged buttes and prehistoric fossil beds make it a scenic must-see, with buffalo herds and a dark-sky program adding adventure.
Sioux Falls – The largest city, named for the Big Sioux River waterfalls in Falls Park.  Visitors can enjoy the cascading falls right downtown, along with a growing arts scene and the Washington Pavilion cultural center.
Tennessee
Nashville – “Music City” known for country music heritage.  Broadway’s honky-tonk bars, the Country Music Hall of Fame, and Grand Ole Opry all make Nashville an immersive live-music experience�.
tripadvisor.com
Memphis – Birthplace of the blues.  Visit Beale Street’s music clubs and Graceland (Elvis Presley’s home).  The city also has the National Civil Rights Museum at the Lorraine Motel and famous barbecue traditions.
Chattanooga – Scenic river city in the mountains.  Its Incline Railway (to Lookout Mountain), Tennessee Aquarium, and riverfront redevelopment are big draws.  Nearby Ruby Falls (underground waterfall) adds a cave adventure.
Gatlinburg (Great Smoky Mountains) – Gatlinburg is the tourist town at the entrance to Great Smoky Mountains National Park.  It offers cabins, mountain views, Ober Gatlinburg ski area, and Ripley’s Aquarium, making it a year-round family destination.
Texas
Austin – The state capital and live-music capital (“Live Music Capital of the World”).  Known for its eclectic culture, outdoor swim spots (Barton Springs), and festivals (South by Southwest, Austin City Limits).  Its food truck and barbecue scene is renowned.
San Antonio – Rich in history, center of Spanish missions (including the Alamo).  The River Walk downtown is a network of riverfront promenades lined with shops and restaurants.  San Antonio also offers theme parks (Six Flags) and Tex-Mex cuisine.
Houston – A sprawling metropolis known for NASA’s Johnson Space Center and diverse food (especially Tex-Mex and Vietnamese).  Its Museum District (fine arts, natural science) and sprawling zoo attract culture and family visitors.
Dallas – A modern urban hub with attractions like the Sixth Floor Museum (JFK), the artsy Dallas Arts District, and nearby Fort Worth’s cowboy heritage (Stockyards).  Dallas’s skyline and shopping (West End) make it a major tourist city.
Utah
Salt Lake City – The capital at the base of the Wasatch Range.  Known for Temple Square (headquarters of the LDS Church) and nearby ski resorts (Big and Little Cottonwood Canyons).  The Great Salt Lake and Bonneville Salt Flats are natural attractions near the city.
Zion National Park (Springdale) – The town of Springdale sits at Zion’s entrance.  Zion’s towering sandstone canyons, emerald pools, and Angels Landing hike are iconic Western landscapes.  Springdale provides lodging, art galleries, and shuttle access into the park.
Arches National Park (Moab) – Moab is the adventure hub for Arches and Canyonlands parks.  Red rock scenery here includes over 2,000 natural stone arches (Delicate Arch) and deep canyons, making it a magnet for hikers and photographers.
Bryce Canyon (Tropic) – Bryce Canyon’s vivid orange hoodoos are unique to southern Utah.  The town of Tropic is a common stop, with guest ranches and the entrance road winding past amphitheaters of hoodoos to sunrise viewpoints.
Vermont
Burlington – The largest city on Lake Champlain.  Burlington’s Church Street pedestrian mall, local craft breweries, and lakeside parks make it a lively small-city visit.  The nearby ferry to New York’s Adirondacks adds to its appeal.
Stowe – A classic ski town at the base of Mt. Mansfield (Vermont’s highest peak).  Stowe also offers mountain biking and fall foliage drives; its quaint village has inns, farm-to-table restaurants, and a brewery.
Woodstock – A picturesque Green Mountain Village known for covered bridges, historic homes (Billings Farm), and the Marsh-Billings-Rockefeller National Historical Park.  It typifies the quintessential Vermont charm.
Montpelier – The state capital (smallest in the U.S.), with a quaint downtown featuring boutique shopping and the gold-domed Vermont State House.  As a cultural center of Central Vermont, it hosts food and arts events.
Virginia
Richmond – The capital with an emerging arts scene.  Its Civil War history is showcased at battlefields and the American Civil War Museum.  The James River Park and C&O Canal towpath offer outdoor activities, and a revived downtown features breweries and galleries.
Virginia Beach – A popular resort city at the Atlantic coast.  Known for its long boardwalk, sandy beaches, and lively oceanfront nightlife.  Nearby First Landing State Park has hiking trails in a maritime forest.
Shenandoah (Luray) – The town of Luray near Shenandoah National Park.  It’s famous for Luray Caverns (marble caves) and as a base for Skyline Drive scenic drives and mountain hikes in the Shenandoahs.
Williamsburg – Historic Colonial Williamsburg recreates 18th-century colonial life.  Travelers also visit the adjacent Jamestown Settlement and Yorktown Battlefield (triad of early American history), making it a top destination for history buffs.
Washington
Seattle – A major metropolis in the Pacific Northwest.  Known for the Space Needle and Pike Place Market (where you can watch “flying fish”), it’s a tech hub with museums (MoPOP) and waterfront ferries on Puget Sound.
Olympic National Park (Port Angeles) – Port Angeles is the visitor gateway.  Olympic offers wildly diverse ecosystems – from rainforests to alpine peaks – with Hurricane Ridge and the Hoh Rainforest among its highlights.
Mount Rainier (Ashford) – Ashford is the west-side gateway to Mount Rainier National Park.  Rainier’s 14,411-foot snow-capped peak dominates the landscape, offering hiking in summer and wildflowers in Paradise Meadows.
Spokane – Eastern Washington’s largest city on the Spokane River.  Landmark Spokane Falls in Riverfront Park anchor its riverfront downtown.  The city also hosts the annual Lilac Festival and serves as a springboard to nearby lakes and Coeur d’Alene, Idaho.
West Virginia
Charleston – The capital on the Kanawha River.  Its Capitol dome (made of aluminum) is iconic.  Visitors enjoy the Avampato Discovery Museum (children’s science and art) and excursions along the river.
Harpers Ferry – Historic town at the confluence of the Potomac and Shenandoah rivers.  Known for John Brown’s raid history, the preserved 19th-century streets (now a national historic park) and hiking the Appalachian Trail.
Snowshoe – A mountain resort (in Pocahontas County) popular for downhill skiing in winter and mountain biking/festivals in summer.  It’s one of the state’s top adventure destinations.
Shepherdstown – A small university town on the Potomac.  It has colonial architecture and book festivals, and it’s near Harpers Ferry for history.  With a scenic river setting, it attracts kayakers and history enthusiasts.
Wisconsin
Milwaukee – The largest city on Lake Michigan’s western shore.  Known for its brewing heritage (Harley-Davidson museum and beer museums), a lively riverwalk, and the Milwaukee Art Museum (noted for its wing-like brise soleil).
Madison – The capital on an isthmus of lakes and home to the University of Wisconsin.  The Capitol building overlooks two lakes, and State Street connects the campus to downtown’s shops and restaurants.  Bike paths and farmers’ markets are abundant.
Door County – A popular summer getaway (the “Cape Cod of the Midwest”) on a peninsula between Green Bay and Lake Michigan.  Towns like Fish Creek and Ephraim offer cherry orchards, art galleries, and waterfront parks.
Wisconsin Dells – A resort city famous for its indoor water parks and natural sandstone “dells” gorge (boat tours).  It brands itself as a family fun destination with theme parks and entertainment.
Wyoming
Jackson (Grand Teton) – The town of Jackson is the gateway to Grand Teton National Park.  Jackson’s town square (and its iconic elk-antler arches) serves as a base for mountain adventures, from Grand Teton summits to wildlife rafting on the Snake River.
Jackson (Yellowstone) – Also a jumping-off point for Yellowstone’s South Entrance (in Grand Teton NP).  Riders can venture into Yellowstone for Old Faithful and Grand Prismatic Spring, then return to Jackson’s vibrant arts and dining scene.
Cheyenne – The state capital with strong Wild West roots.  It hosts Cheyenne Frontier Days (a huge annual rodeo) and has historic train depot museums.  Cheyenne’s Frontier Days Old West Museum showcases regional history.
Cody – Founded by Buffalo Bill Cody, this northern town is a gateway to Yellowstone’s East Entrance.  It has the Buffalo Bill Center of the West (a multi-museum complex) and nightly rodeo in the summer.
